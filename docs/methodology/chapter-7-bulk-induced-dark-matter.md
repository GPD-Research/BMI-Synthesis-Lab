# Chapter 7: Bulk-Induced Dark Matter Effects

## 7.1 Reinterpreting Missing Mass as Tidal Shadowing
In the BMI framework, the 'missing mass' problem is interpreted as an effective gravitational ghost-effect rather than undetected particle matter (WIMPs)[cite: 7]. The hyperspatial shear tensor $S_{\mu\nu}$ exerts a persistent tidal pull on our local spacetime geometry[cite: 7]. The total gravitational potential $\Phi$ is defined as the sum of local baryonic contributions and the induced shadow potential $\Phi_{shadow}$[cite: 7]:
$$\nabla^2 \Phi = 4\pi G (\rho_{baryon} + \rho_{shadow})$$

## 7.2 The Ghost Density Equation
The 'shadow density' $\rho_{shadow}$ is a projection of the adjacent manifold's mass distribution onto our brane[cite: 7]. It is derived by projecting the bulk curvature through the interface thickness $\Sigma$[cite: 7]:
$$\rho_{shadow} = \beta \int [ S_{00} \sqrt{-g} ] dy$$
Here, $\beta$ is the coupling constant calibrated by the local curvature gate $g_{eff}(R)$[cite: 7]. Because $g_{eff}(R) \to 0$ in high-density regions like the solar system ($R \gg R_c$), $\rho_{shadow}$ vanishes, explaining the absence of dark matter effects in local solar system dynamics[cite: 7].

## 7.3 Flattening of Rotation Curves
At galactic scales, where density drops and $g_{eff}(R) \to 1$, the ghost potential becomes significant[cite: 7]. The velocity of a test particle at radius $r$ is[cite: 7]:
$$v^2 = \frac{G M_{baryon}}{r} + \frac{G M_{shadow}}{r_{eff}}$$
As $r$ increases, the shadow contribution $M_{shadow}$ remains correlated to the hyperspatial bridge tilt, forcing $v(r)$ toward a constant value and producing flat rotation curves without requiring dark matter haloes[cite: 7].
